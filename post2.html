The Rhythm of the Akshaya Tritiya

April 18, 2026

Of all the festivals throughout the year, Akshaya Tritiya and Gowri Habba are the ones that really stay with me. I never used to rank these festivals, but from a distance, they’re the traditions I find myself holding onto the most.

My mom has this way of celebrating every festival with so much care, following every tradition and ritual perfectly. But these two stand out to me because they feel so much like her. There’s a certain warmth she brings to them—the house filled with the smell of fresh chandan, and the bright colors of the flowers and mangoes.

Especially with Akshaya Tritiya, everything feels so fresh. The way she celebrates this season is like a slow buildup; right after Ugadi, she sets the Kalasa on the Thadige. It’s a dedicated process—she carefully changes the Kalasa every Tuesday and Friday. I’ve always loved this rhythm because it keeps a fresh, sacred energy alive in the house for weeks.

For those who didn't grow up with this, the "Thadige" (the third day of the lunar month) after Ugadi marks the real start of the summer transition. This tradition goes by many names—depending on where you are, she is called Chaitra Gowri, Nela Gowramma, or Thadige Gowri.

The ritual represents the goddess staying in the house, and the way my mom refreshes the Kalasa keeps that hospitality alive until the final celebration. By the time it is time to move it, she has the doorways lined with fresh mango leaves and the air is thick with the scent of summer.

In the peak of summer, her mango rice, payasa, kosambri, and panaka are the perfect combination, especially with the house smelling of fresh jasmine.

The Recipes

My mom isn’t really the type to have kitchen hacks—she saves her tips and tricks for everything else in life!

Mango Rice

Her mango rice is just mango rice—the classic version everyone knows. To make it, you just use a normal tempering of chana dal, urad dal, hing, curry leaves, peanuts, and turmeric powder. After the peanuts turn crunchy, add the grated mango and turn off the stove, then add salt and rice accordingly.

Akki-Kadlebele Payasa

However, I’d like to include her recipe for Akki-Kadlebele Payasa. It’s a comforting blend of rice and chana dal sweetened with jaggery that rounds off the meal perfectly.

The Method

Soak a handful of chana dal for 20 minutes (preferably in hot water). Cook half a glass of rice and the soaked chana dal together using a 1:3 ratio (1 part rice/dal to 3 parts water). Once cooked, add jaggery (1:1 ratio with the rice) and let it melt completely. Turn off the stove and wait for 5 minutes before adding boiled milk—this pause ensures the milk doesn't curdle from the jaggery. Finish it off with a spoonful of ghee.

It’s a meal that captures the season—simple, bright, and exactly how I remember summer.
